Mandelbrot Fractal


 The Mandelbrot Fractal is a captivating mathematical set known for its intricate and self-replicating patterns. It's generated using the iterative formula f(z) = z*z + c , where z and c are complex numbers. The key idea is to determine whether, after repeatedly applying the formula, the magnitude of the result stays bounded or escapes to infinity. Each point in the complex plane corresponds to a unique fractal image, with different colors representing the number of iterations needed to make the magnitude of f(z) exceed a threshold. The Mandelbrot set is renowned for its endless complexity and beauty, with infinite detail at various scales.

Brownian Motion

Brownian Motion is a fundamental concept in stochastic processes and statistical physics. It describes the random motion of particles suspended in a fluid, such as dust particles in air or pollen in water. Named after the British botanist Robert Brown , who observed this phenomenon in the early 19th century, Brownian Motion is characterized by continuous, erratic movements resulting from countless collisions with surrounding particles. The key features of Brownian Motion include its randomness, unpredictability, and continuous nature. It's mathematically modeled as a stochastic process, often using stochastic differential equations. Brownian Motion plays a crucial role in various scientific disciplines, including physics, chemistry, finance, and biology, serving as a foundational concept for understanding diffusion, molecular motion, and random phenomena. It has practical applications in fields like stock price modeling, particle tracking, and the study of diffusion in biological systems.
